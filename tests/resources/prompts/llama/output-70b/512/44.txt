1. Genetic mutations: Bacteria can develop genetic mutations that make them less susceptible to the effects of antibiotics. For example, some bacteria can produce enzymes that break down antibiotics, rendering them ineffective.
2. Selection pressure: When antibiotics are used extensively, they exert a selective pressure on bacteria to evolve resistance. This means that bacteria that are susceptible to antibiotics are more likely to die off, leaving behind bacteria that are resistant to the antibiotic.
3. Horizontal gene transfer: Bacteria can share genes with each other, including genes that confer antibiotic resistance. This means that even if a bacterium has not been exposed to an antibiotic before, it can still acquire resistance through gene transfer.
