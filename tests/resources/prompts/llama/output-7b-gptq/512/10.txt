### Instruction:
Describe the difference between covalent and ionic bonds, with examples

### Response:
Certainly! Covalent and ionic bonds are two different types of chemical bonds that are formed between atoms in a molecule.

A covalent bond is a bond that is formed between two atoms when they share one or more pairs of electrons. This type of bond is typically found in molecules where the atoms are bonded together by sharing electrons in their outermost energy level, such as in a water molecule (H2O) or a methane molecule (CH4). Covalent bonds are typically stronger than ionic bonds and are more flexible, as the atoms in the bond can move slightly within the molecule.

On the other hand, an ionic bond is a bond that is formed between two atoms when one atom loses an electron and the other atom gains an electron. This type of bond is typically found in molecules where the atoms are held together by the electrostatic attraction between the positively charged atom and the negatively charged atom, such as in table salt (NaCl). Ionic bonds are typically stronger than covalent bonds and are more rigid, as the atoms in the bond are held together by the electrostatic attraction between the positively charged and negatively charged atoms.

Examples of covalent bonds include:

* Hydrogen fluoride (HF)
* Carbon dioxide (CO2)
* Methane (CH4)
* Water (H2O)

Examples of ionic bonds include:

* Sodium chloride (NaCl)
* Calcium carbonate (CaCO3)
* Ammonium nitrate (NH4NO3)
* Sulfuric acid (H2SO4)
