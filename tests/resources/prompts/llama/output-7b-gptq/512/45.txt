Wave-particle duality is a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics that suggests that particles, such as electrons or photons, can exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties depending on how they are observed. This idea was first proposed by Louis de Broglie in 1924 and has been widely accepted and experimentally confirmed since then.

The wave-like properties of particles, such as diffraction and interference, are characteristic of waves, while the particle-like properties, such as definite position and momentum, are characteristic of particles. This duality is significant in quantum mechanics because it challenges our classical understanding of the world, where objects were thought to be either waves or particles, but not both.

In quantum mechanics, the wave-particle duality has important implications for our understanding of the behavior of particles at the atomic and subatomic level.
