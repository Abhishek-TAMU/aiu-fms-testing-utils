### Response:
Thank you for reaching out! Wave-particle duality is a fundamental concept in quantum mechanics that describes the nature of matter and energy at the atomic and subatomic level. In essence, it states that particles, such as electrons, can exhibit both wave-like and particle-like behavior depending on how they are observed.

On one hand, particles can exhibit wave-like properties, such as diffraction and interference, which are characteristic of waves. For example, when electrons pass through a double-slit experiment, they form an interference pattern on a screen, indicating that they are behaving like waves.

On the other hand, particles can also exhibit particle-like properties, such as having a definite position and momentum, which are characteristic of particles. When observed individually, electrons behave like particles, displaying definite positions and momenta.

The significance of wave-particle duality in quantum mechanics lies in its ability to explain the behavior of particles at the atomic and subatomic level. It highlights the idea that matter and energy are not fixed and definite, but rather exist in a state of superposition, where they can exhibit multiple properties simultaneously.

In other words, wave-particle duality shows that particles are not limited to being either waves or particles, but can exhibit both properties depending on the conditions of the experiment. This has far-reaching implications for our understanding of the nature of reality and the behavior of matter and energy at the smallest scales.
