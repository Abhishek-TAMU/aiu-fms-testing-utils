Describe the life cycle of a star, from formation to supernova or black hole

### Response:
Of course! The life cycle of a star is a fascinating process that spans millions or billions of years, depending on the mass of the star. Here's a brief overview of the stages of a star's life:

1. Formation: Stars are formed from giant clouds of gas and dust called nebulae. As these clouds collapse under their own gravity, they begin to spin faster and faster, causing them to flatten into a disk shape. At the center of the disk, a protostar forms, which is a hot, dense ball of gas and dust.
2. Protostar stage: As the protostar continues to collapse, it heats up due to the increasing pressure and gravity. Eventually, it reaches a temperature of about 10 million degrees Celsius, at which point nuclear fusion can occur.
3. Main Sequence: Once the protostar reaches the main sequence, it enters a stable phase of nuclear fusion, during which it fuses hydrogen into helium in its core. This stage can last for billions of years, depending on the mass of the star.
4. Red Giant: As a star runs out of hydrogen fuel in its core, it expands into a red giant, with a radius that can be up to 100 times larger than it was during the main sequence. This stage can last for millions of years.
5. Supernova or Black Hole: Once a star has exhausted its fuel supply, it can either collapse into a black hole or undergo a supernova explosion. In a supernova, the star's core collapses, causing a massive explosion that can be seen from millions of light-years away. In a black hole, the star's gravity becomes so strong that not even light can escape, creating a region of infinite density and zero volume.
